Sweet Spot

Reserve a tennis court ahead of time! Less waiting and more hitting.

View Sweet Spot Prototype

Overview


In the era of public parks, “first come, first serve,” and a lack of common courtesy, securing a sports facility can be tough; players spend more time waiting for a court than actually playing on it. This can be avoided with Sweet Spot, an app that allows players to reserve their courts ahead of time.


Project roles


User research, user experience, and visual design

Problem


Where public parks are the number one solution to play tennis, it becomes a gamble to find an open court. Players may find themselves waiting for up to an hour or driving to different parks to finally find somewhere to play. Having experienced this myself very often, I wanted to explore a solution that could save time and limit down time.


Solution


After conducting the research below and subsequently building upon users’ needs, I developed the concept of Sweet Spot, an app that allows players to reserve tennis courts ahead of time, as well as find others to play with.

Research


I already know that this problem exists because I experience it first hand so I wanted to see if others also consider this a recurring problem as well.


User research


To begin the research process, I sent out a general sports survey to see if this was a universal problem that people experienced.

A few examples of the questions asked are:


  • What sports do you play?
  • How do you pick your facility?
  • Do you reserve it?
  • How long do you wait for your facility?
  • What's stopping you from playing more often and/or longer?
Complete sports facility survey

After sending the survey to my cohort, I received 27 responses. Fortunately, over 50% of the users play tennis and could offer relevant insight. Not only do they have the similar problem but they also experience not having the time to play and not having anyone to play with.

Half the responses are tennis players.

It was worth noting that responders mostly chose their courts based on availability and distance.

While not often, there are instances when finding a court is so difficult that I’ve given up on looking and gone home. I wanted to know if others have done this as well, and six users have given up at least 3 out of 10 times.



On a scale from 1 to 5, 1 being “Probably not,” and 5 being “Heck yes,” over 50% of the users answered 5, saying they would play more often if it were easier to secure a facility.

85% of responses do not reserve their facilities ahead of time.

As a result, players are usually waiting for the next available court.

This solidified that the current method of finding a court needed improvement. The next steps were to find other services that offered something similar to what I wanted to accomplish.

Strengths
  • Simple and straightforward
  • For public parks: free!
Weaknesses
  • Local to SF only
  • Website only, no app, mobile experience isn't ideal
  • Just an online reservation; no physical confirmation/check-in
Opportunities
  • An app would make it more easily accessible
  • Expand to other cities/locations

Threats

Kourts

  • Very well designed

  • Locations are sparse
Opportunities
  • Expand to public parks/other locations

City of Berkeley
Strengths
  • Mobile friendly
  • Familiar checkout system
Weaknesses
  • Pay for public park
  • Pay by the hour
  • Local to Berkeley only
Opportunities
  • Expand to public parks/other locations
  • Provide inexpensive and accessible alternatives
Threats

Each of these methods had its own unique way of reserving court; all were studied to see which flow would best meet this app's needs.

These users, as well as administrators, would require the following functions in order for the app to be successful:

User Stories

  • As a new user I want to sign up for an account with my email
  • As a returning user I want to find a tennis court based on my location
  • As a returning user I want to reserve a court
  • As a returning user I want to view tennis courts on a map
  • As a returning user I want to be able to cancel my court reservation
  • As a returning user I want to find a tennis court that is currently not in use

Branding


The branding began with the ideation of the name of the product which would come after a brainstorm of all the tennis terms that would come to mind. As soon as I got to “sweet spot,” which refers to the spot on the racquet that minimizes force on the hands on ball impact, I knew it was the perfect name because it could serve as a double entendre for “the perfect location.”



Once I had the name, I was able to visualize the logo: an “S” or two over a ball to look like a tennis ball, or the words “sweet spot” over a tennis racquet. After feedback that two S’s would sort of resemble a Nazi symbol, I strayed away from that idea and finalized one S which would end up looking very similar to a tennis ball.



The colors were inspired by some of the grand slams and the bright green from a tennis ball. The later mockups would end up not passing accessibility standards so some of the colors had to be changed, but still were inspired by the grand slams.



The fonts were found using Fontpair.co, both trying to mimic the font found in the Australian Open logo. One is used for the logo and title fonts, and the other is used for the body font.

After implementing those change, I felt like I had a real MVP for this app.

Conclusion


While I am satisfied with the research process put into this app, given more time, I would have done a second competitive analysis on apps and services that match you with tennis partners.


Doing a project so personal to me made it feel like the project was creating itself. The user research was a lot of my tennis peers nodding in agreement and wishing an app like this already existed. I welcomed the constructive feedback very warmly and the engagement with the product made me more excited to improve upon and work on it.


The drawback to an app like this is that, in theory, it makes sense. However, its real time application is questionable - even a user test response asked, “How effective will this be when reserving public courts?” Convincing local governments to use and monitor new locks for the courts could prove difficult.


The idea for a tennis court reservation app was an idea I had during frustration amidst finding, or failing to find, a tennis court during a busy weekend. While this is a proof of concept, I hope it’s an idea that can become a reality and developing this prototype is a step towards that.

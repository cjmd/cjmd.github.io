Clout Storage

A cloud storage service that organizes your photos using machine learning and AI.


Overview


Without a clear vision, a client requested a product that was similar to existing cloud storage services. Clout Storage, a cloud storage photo app, implements features that use the same features but is geared toward photo and social media use.


Project roles


User research, User Experience, and Visual Design

Problem

The client requested for a service to tap into the cloud storage market, citing services such as Google Drive, Dropbox, Box, Evernote, and Pinterest as potential competitors. However, the client had no clear vision for the product, with no name, branding, and even a target audience in mind. The client only had a list of requested features: saving content, organizing content, creating content, uploading content, and sharing content to encourage the growth of users.

Solution

With the research and process outlined below, the product developed is called Clout Storage, a cloud storage photo app geared toward phooto and social media use.

Research


The client requested for a service to tap into the cloud storage market, citing services such as Google Drive, Dropbox, Box, Evernote, and Pinterest as potential competitors. However, the client had no clear vision for the product and without a name, branding, and even a target audience, the first step was to do research.


User research


A survey was conducted to gather information on how users use existing cloud storage services. While only 35 users took the survey, their responses were informative of current products and how they're used.

A few examples of the questions asked are:


  • What does "cloud storage" mean to you?
  • Do you use cloud storage?
  • Which cloud storage service do you use?
  • What do you use our cloud storage service(s) for?
  • What are your favorite features of cloud storage?
  • What do you dislike about cloud storage?

When asked what cloud storage is, all participants understand that it's a drive used to store and access content remotely and online. When asked to provide examples, the participants listed the biggest names in the market: Google Drive, iCloud, Dropbox, OneDrive, and Box. Other examples provided were enterprise services like Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud Platform, and Amazon Web Services. These particular examples gave me insight on the different uses of cloud services.

Out of the 35 participants, only two (2) don’t use cloud storage.

Out of all of the participants who do use cloud storage, all but one (1) use Google Drive. Majority use iCloud and Dropbox as well.

The participants who do use cloud storage use them for a variety of reasons as expected. Of all the auxiliary reasons to use cloud storage, photos had the biggest use, which makes sense in this day and age when taking photos is easily done so we’re taking a LOT of them and need somewhere to store them.

Seeing the variety of use cases for cloud storage introduces many directions that this app could take. Instead of serving multiple purposes and competing with the big names, this app could be geared towards a certain niche.

After collecting data on which services were used most, competitive analyses were made between them.

Competitive Analysis


Google Drive

Strengths
  • First 15 GB of storage are free
  • Google product syncing
  • Offers up to 30 TB of storage
  • Easy to upload and organize
Weaknesses
  • Doesn't offer unlimited storage
Opportunities
  • Offers unlimited storage
Threats
  • Business users might opt for unlimited storage

iCloud Drive
Strengths
  • 5 GB of free storage to start
  • Automatic upload for those with more than one Apple device
Weaknesses
  • Not much use outside of Apple devices
  • Organization isn't as straightforward and intuitive
Opportunities
  • Available automation for other devices/platforms
Threats
  • Users might opt for services that live outside the Apple ecosystem

Dropbox
Strengths
  • Get 2 GB of space for free
  • Unlimited storage for enterprise use
Weaknesses
  • Free storage is smaller than other services
  • No smart organization
  • Forces download of desktop app upon onboarding
Opportunities
  • Higher free storage
Threats
  • Users might opt for services that sync across other products

After seeing that all services do well in their own right, their processes were studied to gather the general flow and patterns of how onboarding, saving content, and organizing work.



User personas


Once the main processes were more grounded, I returned to the survey to find the personas that would be using (or not using) this service: the nonuser, the regular user, and the power user.

Goals

Frustrations

27, Social media influencer


Motivations

Goals

Frustrations

The Power User

Kevin Scout
31, Software engineer


Motivations
  • Wants to use all the services available


Goals

Frustrations

User stories

  • As a new user I want to sign up for an account with my email
  • As a new user I want a tutorial on how to use the service
  • As a returning user I want to paste to save content from my computer
  • As a returning user I want to upload content from my computer
  • As a returning user I want to create folders
  • As a returning user I want to tag content

  • As a returning user I want to move content

  • As a returning user I want to search content by name
  • As a returning user I want to search content by tags
  • As a returning user I want to search content by context
  • As a returning user I want to re-download content to my computer

After solidifying the user stories I now had a clearer picture of what the user flows would look like for the product.

Content Strategy

With this content strategy, wireframes were now able to bbe formed and made into a clickable prototype.

The wireframe and prototype were modeled after existing services so that users would have no problem identifying what to do. This is reflective in the user tests that were conducted.

These tests provided a lot of feedback for me, like one user's list view suggestion. There were a lot of screens that the "regular users" weren't able to use; that was my lack of direction for them. I should have been more specific with what "organizing content" meant.

With that feedback, the user flows and processes were satisfactory and the next step was branding in order to create higher quality mockups.

Branding

If this cloud storage service remained a generic service that's supposed to compete with Google Drive, Dropbox, and iCloud, it would get lost in such a saturated market. I revisited the survey results and the user personas to see if there was a product that could go to a less saturated market. After realizing that majority of users use cloud storage for photos, I thought of different ways that a cloud storage service could be used for photos other than just storage.

Because of the surgence of photos and social media in the past decade, it made sense to come up with a service for this purpose. Eventually I landed on an idea of a cloud storage service that was geared towards social media users, bloggers, and influencers to use for their photos.



Because this is now geared toward the "influencer," I think the color palette should be a little more feminie, light, and airy but still approachable for everyone.

Initial mockups

The first versions of my mockups had few screens and, as seen in the dashboard above, had inconsistencies with sizing, spacing, and overall looks to them, as well as UI issues, like back buttons and navigation issues. After that feedback, I was able to create more screens for more processes and implement the recommended feedback. See the improved dashboard below.

The improved desktop mockups were made into a clickable prototype which was tested using Invision. All three users who tested the prorotype said the app was clear and clean. There was some confusion with the "new," "add," and "upload" buttons as a new user so improvements were needed there. The bulk actions or "Select" tool should be usable and the "sort" button should be usable to see how the pictures can be sorted. Lastly, the dashboard or home should have more context rather than just a "junk drawer" of pictures; albums, memories, or tags at a glance as well as recently uploaded pictures.

After the feedback from the user tests, I was able to implement the changes which brought me to the final prototype, which can be seen below.

Conclusion


This project was a little daunting to start not only because of the lack of vision and direction given by the client but also because of how saturated the market is that the client was trying to enter. Competing with Dropbox and Google Drive is difficult as is, let alone as a product with no target audience.

The research conducted definitely helped, especially when figuring out the direction of the product. However, after the pivot from a generic cloud storage service to a photo storage service, the additional research wasn't conducted for a photo storage service, including competitive analysis for existing products in the same space. If that research were completed, I would have better insight as to which features were necessary and which were extraneous for this product.

The feedback I received from external sources has also been really helpful because I tended to get stuck staring at the project without much evolution. Without the right feedback, I would still be trying to create a competitor to big name cloud storage services.

If given more time, I would have conducted the extra research on the niche market. I would also have touched more upon the creaiton of documents, for blog posts or post captions, and what collaboration would look like.

While I feel like I've improved so much doing this project alone, the biggest takeaway is how much feedback matters. Constantly getting comments from graders, my mentor, and my peers really helped put this project together. Moving forward, I will be sure to gather as much external feedback as I possibly can.
